PREFACE

The aim of this guide is to represent the basic tactics, techniques, procedures and technical information used by all Australian Armed Forces [ARMA 3] (AAF) personnel, in tasks appropriated upon them as members of an AAF section which is part of a coherent platoon in the online simulator ARMA 3.

DISCLAIMER

This guide has been written by a multitude of members over the time period of several years, it is a constant work in progress in what is relevant to the simulated battlespace. It has not been copied, ripped or sourced from anything outside of the AAF.

(1-2) FIELD CRAFT

Let your plans be dark and as impenetrable as night, and when you move, fall like a thunderbolt - Sun Tzu, The Art of War

All AAF soldiers are rifleman, first and foremost. As a rifleman, your responsibilities are:

Know the mission
  • The mission objective,
  • the rules of engagement,
  • the names of all members in your unit, and
  • all mission call signs.
Listen to your section leader (NEED REVISION)
  • Know your place,
  • Unless the section leader has asked for opinions, don't give them
Situation Awareness
  • Cover your arc,
  • Don't cross lanes of fire,
  • Maintain knowledge of friendly forces in the area, and
  • Identify friend from foe.

There are many ways to detect targets, we cover seven of them here and how you can use them to stay concealed.

Silhouette Contrasting with your background
Shadows Darkened environments show less
Shape You will not find straight edges in nature
Sound The sound of breathing or a twig snapping
Spacing Nature is irregular
Surface Nature is rugged, and rarely shiny
Movement Human vision naturally snaps to movement

Always keep a low profile, if your at the halt, use your initiative, go crouched. Always make sure you:

  • can look through your concealment and cover,
  • avoid isolated cover, e.g. not the only bush in the field,
  • use covered routes e.g. dead ground,
  • move slowly to avoid attracting attention, and
  • blend in with your surroundings.

In order for your team to recognize a target, you must be able to effectively communicate it. Target indications should follow a simple format, and if all else fails, remember the following:

  • distance,
  • direction, and
  • description.

There are several methods to indicate direction:

  • Relative, e.g FRONT, RIGHT, 1/2 LEFT, REAR.
  • Reference, e.g LEFT OF THE 4th HANGAR.
  • Compass, e.g NORTH EAST, SOUTH, 290 degrees SW.

Each of these methods have pitfalls in different situations.

  • The relative method will fail if there is no established axis, and
  • an accurate compass reading can take longer to identify during a firefight.

(1-3) FORMATIONS

An army is a team. It lives,eats, sleeps, fights as a team. This individuality stuff is a bunch of bullshit - General George Patton

Section formations depend on two major factors:

  • the environment and situation that the section is in, and
  • the direction from which enemy fire is expected.

The section formations commonly used are:

  • single file;
  • arrowhead; and
  • extended line.

The single file formation is the standard infiltration formation.

  • It is highly defensive to the flanks,
  • effective in low visibility,
  • low profile, and
  • has limited front/rear protection.

The arrowhead is the standard patrol formation.

  • It is defensive to the front,
  • naturally assumed, and
  • best used in open areas.

The extended line is the standard assault formation.

  • It is highly defensive to the front and rear,
  • easy to pivot to a new direction,
  • difficult to maintain, and
  • best used with flank protection.

(1-4) FIELD DRILLS

Individuals play the game, but teams beat the odds - US NAVY Seals motto

The basic drill is a simple survival procedure, it increases the survivability of the soldier under fire. The sequence of the basic drill is as follows:

  • run,
  • down,
  • crawl,
  • aim,
  • fire.

The contact drill is the basic reaction of the section to enemy fire, it may be altered due to situation, but ultimately aims to:

  • regain the initiative, and
  • force the enemy into submission with a large volume of fire.

The sequence of actions on a contact drill are as follows:

  • The forwardmost members of the section return fire using an entire magazine (panic mag), whilst performing the basic drill.
  • The guns move to an overwatch position and begin suppressing the contact direction.
  • The remainder of the section performs the basic drill and prepares to either:
    • flank or close with the enemy,
    • watch and shoot until further orders, and or
    • provide covering fire for the section to break contact.

If the section is unable to cope, the section must break contact. The sequence of actions for a break contact are as follows:

  • The guns place down suppression fire, with battle pairs bounding rearwards,
  • Once all battle pairs are firm, the guns will bound rearwards, with the section providing compensating suppressive fire, and
  • The previous two actions are repeated until out of the contact zone or the initiative has been regained.

On identification of an impassable or dangerous obstacle (e.g. a road or fence), and the obstacle is to be passed, the sequence of actions are as follows:

  • The section must sets up an all round defense,
  • The guns must move up to the obstacle and deploy watching the flanks.
  • Scouts must cross the obstacle, clear the obstacle and communicate back to the section commander the 'all clear'.
  • The remainder of the section will then pass the obstacle, and set up an round defense,
  • Members of the section will then deploy watching the flanks, while the guns pass the obstacle.
  • Once all the section is across, the section continues on.

A common shifting maneuvre is the Australian peel. The sequence of actions for a peel are as follows:

  • Depending on the axis of movement of the peel, the last man in the formation will proceed to the opposing end of the section,
  • The remainder of the section repeats step (a) until the entire formation has shifted the required distance.

NEEDS REVISION

When the section in contact is required to alter facing, a pivot is performed. The sequence of actions are as follows:

  • Depending on the direction of the pivot, the formation will re-orient to face the new direction.
  • The member of the formation closest to the direction of pivot will hold position, and act as a pivot for the rest of the formation to swing around on.
  • The guns must move up to their position within the oriented formation, and call out guns up when ready.
  • The remainder of the section will then move to their new positions within the formation, calling last man when the last man is across.

0 The section attack is the continuing attack on a enemies position after a contact drill, repaper-meditated attack on an enemy position. The following actions must occur when preparing for a section attack:

  • Acquisition of en disposition, risk assessment,
  • Re-distribution of ammunition within the section by the 2IC,
  • Reconnaissance of the target is made, and
  • Orders given out to the section.

Once the section attack begins, the section commander and 2IC will be controlling the rate of movement and fire and coordinating fire and support elements. Responsibilities of the infantry man during a section attack is to make sure:

  • you stay in formation,
  • you maintain your designated rate of fire,
  • inform the section when you are reloading so compensation can be made, and
  • do not converge on the target.

2 After finishing the section attack,and a fight through has been made, the following actions occur to maintain the security and well being of the section.

  • Section commander calls all round defense - ensuring battlefield clearance,
  • The 2IC assesses section status and may redistribute ammunition, and
  • Organization of a CASEVAC and any captured POW's dealt with.

3 Handling prisoners-of-war in ARMA 3 is uncommon,but is possible so one must be ready to perform the following actions in that situation.

  • Search and disarm the PW - identify the PW if possible,
  • Ensure surveillance of the PW is maintained at all times, and
  • Ensure that security is maintained at all times.

(1-5) PATROLLING

Thus, what enables the wise sovereign and the good general to strike and conquer, and achieve things beyond the reach of ordinary men, is fore knowledge - Sun Tzu, The Art of War

Patrolling is the method of which we can establish contact with the enemy, to seize and hold ground and to provide security. Other aims of a patrol can be the following,

  • To gain or retain the initiative,
  • To gain information on tenement,
  • To prevent the enemy closing on friendly positions,
  • To deny the enemy gaining information, and
  • To harass the enemy.

There exists different types of patrols, each holding its own particular objectives, these types are displayed below.

  • Reconnaissance Patrols,
  • Fighting Patrols, and
  • Standing Patrols.

There are different types of fighting patrols, some examples are the following, and can be expected when given an order to conduct a fighting patrol.

  • Search and destroy,
  • Raid or snatch and grab,
  • Ambush, and
  • Escort.

There are different conditions for standing patrols, some of these are shown below are the following, and can be expected when given an order to conduct a standing patrol.

  • A standing patrol will be forward of a weakly defended area,
  • on likely enemy approaches,and
  • with a ready reaction force on standby.

While on a patrol, the foremost responsibilities of the patrol man are to keep a heightened sense of situational awareness, the patrol may be long and dull, but you must be alert at all times.Important things to remember are:

  • The patrol man should know where everyone is in the formation,
  • Speed and sound must be minimal, always avoid silhouetting,
  • Arcs of responsibility must be covered at all times,
  • If the patrol stops at any time, find a position of cover and watch your arc,
  • Know the mission plan and likely enemy strength and activities.

(1-6) AMBUSHES

They are in front of us, behind us, and we are flanked on both sides by an enemy that outnumbers us 29:1. They can't get away from us now! - Lewis B. "Chesty" Puller, USMC

A deliberate ambush aims to kill or capture the enemy, disrupt enemy movements, to gain information and or provide security. Locations and situations for when an ambush may be planned are as follows:

  • Known enemy routes,
  • Areas where vegetation changes,
  • Possible lines of withdrawal after successful attack on enemy position, and
  • Approaches to settled areas or caches.

Some basic considerations to be taken when planning an ambush is that thorough planning is made by the commander, and that there is a semicircles cut plan. Other factors include:

  • Sound intelligence,
  • Available camouflage and concealment, and
  • Maximum firepower.

In preparation for an ambush, the following actions must be adopted for success of the ambush, and to maintain the security of the section.

  • Preparation of mines, claymores, trip flares,
  • Distribution of orders, method of springing, action of compromise, method of withdrawal,
  • Movement to ambush location,
  • Final recce of area and modification of orders in adjustment to situation, and
  • Ambush set.

(1-7) COMMUNICATIONS

Never tell people how to do things. Tell them what to do and they will surprise you with their ingenuity - George S. Patton

Communications are exchanges of information by two or more parties. The information must be transmitted and received/understood. You must know how to communicate with your leaders and fellow soldiers. You must be able to tell:

  • what you see,
  • what you are doing,
  • what you have done,
  • what you are going to do, and
  • what you need.

There are several means of communications. Each has its own capabilities, advantages, and disadvantages. Those you can use are described in this section.Global Chat (Grey): This will not be utilized in game by anyone during an operation, it should be the direct result of only in-game messages such as deaths andconnectionreports.Side chat (Blue): This is for communication between the Section Leaders and Fire team leaders. Both elements may utilize this channel to conduct movement and contactreports.Group chat (Green): This is the main channel used by your group. Where the team Leader conveys all commands as well as any other communication to their team.Vehicle chat (Yellow): This channel is utilized when you jump into a vehicle. This channel has to be utilized when your team jumps into a vehicle that has members from a different team piloting or riding in it.DirectCommunication (White): This channel is simulated voice communication with your immediate area. It is limited in how far you can talk,so you will have to be close to them to communicate.

Speak clearly, slowly, and in natural phrases. Enunciate each word. If a receiving operator must write the message, allow time for him to do so; listen before transmitting, to avoid interfering with other transmissions.

(1-8) NAVIGATION

Perpetual optimism is a force multiplier - Colin Powell

Compasses are the primary navigation tools to use when moving in an outdoor world where there is no other way to find directions.

An intersection is the location of an unknown point by successively occupying at least two(preferably three) known positions on the ground and then map sighting on the unknown location. The steps for a intersection areas follows:

  • Move to a known position, and check your location via a re-section,
  • Find the bearing to the target,
  • Draw a line on the map along the straight edge,
  • Repeat this step for each known position, and
  • The intersection of the lines on the map is the location of the unknown point.

A resection is the location of your own position by successively finding at least two(preferably three) known positions on the ground and then map sighting then to find your location. The steps for a resection areas follows:

  • Locate at least two known distant locations or prominent features on the ground and mark the on the map,
  • Find the bearing to one of the known positions,
  • Draw a line on the map along the straight edge,
  • Repeat this step for each known position, and
  • The intersection of the lines on the map is your location.

(1-9) BRIEFING

(Quote)

The following guidelines provide the format for a full pre-mission briefing, and what other relevant information to include. It is based upon the default Australian army SMEAC format, and has been shortened down to what is completely relevant to the ARMA 3 environment. When writing a briefing, the most important factor is to keep it simple, stupid (KISS).

Situation

  • Topography
    • Use of maps, air photographs, visual reconnaissance and intelligence
  • Enemy/Friendly Forces
    • Activities,
    • Equipment,
    • Location/s,
    • Routes,
    • Size
  • Neutral Forces
    • Movement of local inhabitants and civilians in the AO

Mission

  • Who, what, when, where and why

Execution

  • General outline and type of patrol
    • Phase n
    • Timings
    • Locations
    • Routes
    • Boundaries (TAOR)
    • RV procedure
    • Degrees of weapons readiness (DOWR)
    • Rehearsals
    • Actions on
  • Phase 1 - n

Administration and Log

  • Equipment (Maps, radios etc)
  • Special equipment (Laser markers, satchel charges etc)
  • Weapons and ammunition
  • Equipment and special equipment
  • Medical

Command and Signals

  • Call signs (C/S)
  • Order of seniority
  • Radio
    • Radio checks
    • Radio silence
    • Actions on lost communications

Key things to remember is when first starting off your SMEAC briefing, give the layout and description of the AO, maybe include a map depending on how unknown it is; and ensure everyone knows where their going. Make notes of whether you can expect heavy weather and fog, will the nights be heavy lit and therefore maybe considerations for night vision may not be needed. In general: where your going, obstacles (creeks, roads), key terrain and only describe ground relevant to the mission.Note whether we are against a completely hostile enemy and civilian populace, or will they're be a neutral go-between, are they alerted to our presence or not; and what kind of regular activity should we expect, what should we suspect. Mention enemy strength, possible unit numbers and armaments, possible strong points and areas to avoid when describing the situation, make it clear and short, summarizing and dot points are good for this.

The mission line must be kept as simple as possible, effectively short and sweet, a good example would be: 1 Section is to clear TAOR Alpha NLT 1800h 11 June 2006, IOT allow A Coy to set up C.HQ

Execution is best kept in dot points, and depending on mission, keep away from depth and general. Include brief synopsis of phase and what signifies the end of the phase. Remember the degrees of readiness (empty, load, action, instant) and remember these do apply to ARMA 3. If we're in a non-hostile environment you shouldn't be walking around with your gun up; and remember empty signifies no magazine loaded, you still carry ammunition though.

Describe the weapons and ammunition, and perhaps reference back to the enemy situation, if you may be facing a light armored vehicle patrol that's reported in the TAOR, then packing a anti-tank or ordnance may be a wise up. Ensure everyone has their watches, and scouts and or section commanders carry there maps and radios; and any other essential equipment. Everyone should know their first - aid by now, but if its a bit unsure, run through how to pull a team-mate out of the heat of battle and get him patched up.

Note the location of the platoon headquarters, friendly command positions and where you can relax for some R+R, also note down any forward operation bases and out posts.

Call signs should be recorded prior to action, and if possible their contents also, leaders should be decided between ranking members prior to action and depending on turnout, specific section positions designated.

Communicate the intent clearly. Instead of giving instructions that are understandable, give instructions they can't misunderstand.
